I Wandered Lonely as a Cloud: Analyzing Wordsworth's Poem and His Nature Enjoyment

Oct 23, 2024 • By Cen • Book Reviews

Introduction

“And then my heart with pleasure fills,
And dances with the daffodils.” – William Wordsworth

"I Wandered Lonely as a Cloud," is a celebrated poem by the English Romantic poet William Wordsworth. Composed in 1804 and published in 1807, this poem captures the essence of nature's beauty and its profound impact on the human spirit. The poem narrates Wordsworth's chance encounter with a field of daffodils by a lake, an experience that fills him with joy and inspires a sense of companionship with the flowers. The daffodils, with their fluttering and dancing, become a symbol of the poet's own inner joy and the enduring memory that rekindles his spirits even when he is alone or in a pensive mood. The poem is a testament to the transformative power of nature and its ability to uplift the human soul, reflecting Wordsworth's deep connection with the natural world and his belief in the healing and inspiring qualities of the countryside.

Themes

  • Nature's healing power
  • Contrast the riverside with urban life
  • Solitude and Connection

What is Daffodils?

Daffodils, or Narcissus, are spring-blooming flowers known for their vibrant yellow and white colors. Native to the Mediterranean, these resilient plants symbolize rebirth and are celebrated for their cheerful appearance, often heralding the arrival of spring.

Who is Wordsworth?

William Wordsworth (1770-1850) was a prominent English Romantic poet. Born in the picturesque Lake District of England, he is renowned for his deep connection with nature and his celebration of the beauty found in the simplicity of rural life. Wordsworth's poetry, characterized by its lyrical quality and focus on the emotional response to nature, played a pivotal role in the Romantic Movement. His work, often written in accessible language, aimed to evoke a sense of wonder and the sublime in the reader. Alongside Samuel Taylor Coleridge, Wordsworth co-published "Lyrical Ballads" in 1798, a collection that marked the beginning of the Romantic Age in English literature. His poetry also delved into themes of childhood, innocence, and the power of imagination, reflecting his belief in the healing and transformative qualities of nature. Wordsworth's influence on literature and his contributions to the Romantic era continue to be celebrated and studied today.

Analysis - Wordsworth's Appreciation to the Nature

The companionship of Nature gives Wordsworth a sense of appreciation. He wandered into the field alone, yet the daffodils were “dancing” around him. His use of language personalizes the daffodils as his friends, companying him with the warmth by the lake place, within nature's embrace. Wordsworth reveled in the scene, where his worries and thoughts vanished in Nature. In his eyes, the “host” of daffodils became countless, just like the “continuous … stars” in the “Milky Way.” He emphasizes the immense number of daffodils he encountered, symbolizing the boundlessness of nature and linking to his admiration and appreciation for it. At the time the poem was written, most people were inclined to think of controlling nature due to the success of the Industrial Revolution; however, Wordsworth persistently advocated respect and appreciation for Nature, embodying a strong symbol of Romanticism. When daffodils became the “Milky Way,” they epitomized something beyond the poet’s imagination. The limitless magnitude of nature offers ample space for humans to explore, enhancing the poet’s notion of appreciating Nature instead of seeking to control it. Moreover, the power of nature makes Wordsworth more grateful to it. At that point, he discovered real happiness. He was in “glee,” feeling “gay,” and part of “jocund company.” Those diversified word choices all serve to prove his joy. It was nature that transformed him from “solitude” to “jocund,” thus leading him to fully appreciate nature.

Nature pulled Wordsworth out of modern life. In modern life, people are eager for money and rush to work, especially at his age when society had just completed the Industrial Revolution, and people tend to engage in more intensive work for production. The poet “wandered” near the lake and “saw a crowd,” a bevy of daffodils. They were depicted as lovely, tranquil, and supportive, contrasting with the “crowd” in human society. He was kind of gratitude for leaving the society, standing in nature, and being distant from the chaos, which gave him respite from the exhausting society that had left him lonely. The initial loneliness and the later happiness have formed a contrast between the highly developed society, which creates automatic isolation among people, and the pristine field, where everything unfolds naturally with warmth.

Being “vacant” or “pensive,” the influence of nature is always profound, benefiting people from both inner and outer sides; similarly, Wordsworth, as the paragon of romanticism, has a significant impact on people from different perspectives both within and outside of society. He was trying to convey his findings of nature from the external to influence the internal, encouraging citizens to start or continue to find relaxation and happiness in nature admist their tough modern work. After immersing himself in nature, he gradually discovered his happiness and companionship, which had been obscured in that society. From the sky to the land, from the city to the countryside, and from society to nature, the sequence of contrast underscores the importance of nature, reinforcing Wordsworth’s appreciation for being drawn away from the isolated and somewhat capitalized world. He reflected on the differences between the two again, “gazed,” and surprisingly discovered beauties in nature that he had not had a chance to notice in society. After his profound experience with those daffodils, he had been rejuvenated from his overwhelmed life, returning to look “inward” of himself again, aligning perfectly with Romanticism, which emphasizes individuals. Thus, the dramatic change he underwent made him more appreciative to nature.
