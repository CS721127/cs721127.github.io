I Wandered Lonely as a Cloud:
Analyzing Wordsworth's Nature Enjoyment

A Romantic reading of the "Daffodils" poem

Oct 23, 2024· By Cen· Book Reviews

Introduction

"And then my heart with pleasure fills,
And dances with the daffodils."
— William Wordsworth

"I Wandered Lonely as a Cloud," is a celebrated poem by the English Romantic poet William Wordsworth. Composed in 1804 and published in 1807, this poem captures the essence of nature's beauty and its profound impact on the human spirit. The poem narrates Wordsworth's chance encounter with a field of daffodils by a lake, an experience that fills him with joy and inspires a sense of companionship with the flowers.

The daffodils, with their fluttering and dancing, become a symbol of the poet's own inner joy and the enduring memory that rekindles his spirits even when he is alone or in a pensive mood. The poem is a testament to the transformative power of nature and its ability to uplift the human soul, reflecting Wordsworth's deep connection with the natural world.

Themes

  • Nature's healing power
  • Contrast between the riverside and urban life
  • Solitude and Connection

What are Daffodils?

Daffodils, or Narcissus, are spring-blooming flowers known for their vibrant yellow and white colors. Native to the Mediterranean, these resilient plants symbolize rebirth and are celebrated for their cheerful appearance, often heralding the arrival of spring.

Who is Wordsworth?

William Wordsworth (1770–1850) was a prominent English Romantic poet. Born in the picturesque Lake District of England, he is renowned for his deep connection with nature and his celebration of the beauty found in the simplicity of rural life. Wordsworth's poetry, characterized by its lyrical quality and focus on the emotional response to nature, played a pivotal role in the Romantic Movement. Alongside Samuel Taylor Coleridge, Wordsworth co-published "Lyrical Ballads" in 1798, marking the beginning of the Romantic Age in English literature.

Analysis — Wordsworth's Appreciation of Nature

The companionship of Nature gives Wordsworth a sense of appreciation. He wandered into the field alone, yet the daffodils were "dancing" around him. His use of language personalizes the daffodils as his friends, companying him with the warmth by the lake place, within nature's embrace. Wordsworth reveled in the scene, where his worries and thoughts vanished in Nature.

In his eyes, the "host" of daffodils became countless, just like the "continuous … stars" in the "Milky Way." He emphasizes the immense number of daffodils he encountered, symbolizing the boundlessness of nature and linking to his admiration and appreciation for it. At the time the poem was written, most people were inclined to think of controlling nature due to the success of the Industrial Revolution; however, Wordsworth persistently advocated respect and appreciation for Nature, embodying a strong symbol of Romanticism.

Nature pulled Wordsworth out of modern life. In modern life, people are eager for money and rush to work, especially at his age when society had just completed the Industrial Revolution, and people tend to engage in more intensive work for production. The poet "wandered" near the lake and "saw a crowd," a bevy of daffodils. They were depicted as lovely, tranquil, and supportive, contrasting with the "crowd" in human society.

Being "vacant" or "pensive," the influence of nature is always profound, benefiting people from both inner and outer sides. Similarly, Wordsworth, as the paragon of Romanticism, has a significant impact on people from different perspectives both within and outside of society. He was trying to convey his findings of nature from the external to influence the internal, encouraging citizens to start or continue to find relaxation and happiness in nature amidst their tough modern work.

After his profound experience with those daffodils, he had been rejuvenated from his overwhelmed life, returning to look "inward" of himself again — aligning perfectly with Romanticism, which emphasizes individuals. Thus, the dramatic change he underwent made him more appreciative of nature.
