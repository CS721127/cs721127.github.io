A Doll's House:
Discussion on the Feasibility of Nora's Leave

A literary analysis of gender, freedom, and inner awakening

Nov 5, 2024· By Cen· Book Reviews

Introduction

"I believe that I am first and foremost a human being, like you — or anyway, that I must try to become one."
— Henrik Ibsen, A Doll's House

"A Doll's House" by Henrik Ibsen is a groundbreaking play that critiques societal norms and gender roles in the 19th century. The story revolves around Nora Helmer, a seemingly content housewife who is secretly hiding a past where she defied societal expectations to save her husband's life. As the play unfolds, Nora's husband, Torvald, treats her more like a child or a pet than an equal partner, reflecting the patriarchal society they live in.

When a creditor threatens to expose Nora's forgery of a signature to secure a loan, she is forced to confront the reality of her position and the true nature of her relationship with Torvald. The climax comes when Torvald's reaction to the crisis reveals his concern for his reputation over his wife, prompting Nora to question her entire life and the values she has held dear. In a dramatic finale, Nora decides to leave her husband and children, seeking self-discovery and independence.

Themes

  • Gender Roles and Feminism
  • Individualism vs. Social Expectations
  • Marriage and Partnership
  • Truth and Deception
  • Freedom and Self-Discovery

Author — Henrik Ibsen

Henrik Ibsen (1828–1906) is widely recognized as a titan of modern drama and the "father of realism" in theater. Born in Skien, Norway, Ibsen's work is marked by its moral analysis set against a realistic middle-class backdrop, featuring penetrating dialogue and rigorous thought. His plays, such as "A Doll's House," "Ghosts," and "Hedda Gabler," broke with traditional conventions, exploring existential themes and societal critiques.

Analysis — The Feasibility and Real Significance of Nora's Leave

Ibsen's A Doll's House is a quintessential social problem play that depicts the story of a housewife, trapped like a bird in a cage, awakening to her consciousness and resolutely leaving home, thereby raising the social issue of exploring the path to women's liberation. The final slamming door of the play resonates like thunder from Northern Europe to China, creating a series of "Nora-type" new female images. However, in his lecture "What Happens After Nora Leaves?", Lu Xun mentioned the limitations that Nora, as a woman of her time, faced in terms of economic foundations and the inevitability of her either falling into depravity or returning. Yet, if we return to the work and focus on the context of the times, Nora was not without options; instead, she could pave a wider path.

Nora is a woman with an independent consciousness and a rebellious spirit. This independence and rebellion lay the foundation for her not returning home. Although she has always been treated by Helmer as a "bird in a cage," she still retains her stubbornness and resistance in all aspects of life. When she learns that her husband is ill and cannot raise enough funds, she does not accept the coercion and temptation of Krogstad, but resolutely takes the responsibility and guilt of borrowing money under a false name. This shows Nora's deep-seated adherence to principles and is not a role that simply follows the orders of men.

The social background and the rise of feminism at the time laid a good foundation for Nora's departure. Feminism provided a good social foundation for women to leave their families independently and made people care more about women's lives. The character of Mrs. Lin Dan's self-reliance in the play proves the possibility of women living independently in that era. Norwegian scholar Dai Li pointed out that women were allowed to participate in private liberal clubs. Women in that era were gradually given more and more free rights, and they began to have their own lives.

At the same time, considering its impact on other countries, Nora's departure has the highest value in any case. This spirit will be applied to internal social changes, extending its influence not only to Norway but also to every corner of society, such as China. At that time in China, Nora's spirit reflected a kind of people's inner awakening and rebellious ideas. Hu Shi's "Ibsenization" and individualism also reflected the needs of Chinese society at that time.

Ibsen once said: "Asking questions is my business, but I don't have the answers." Uncertainty still exists, and we cannot get an accurate answer from the work itself.

Based on this, Nora's departure will not plunge her into the abyss, but will make the path of independence and autonomy wider and explore more possibilities. Good social conditions and a positive spirit make Nora's departure the right choice. The "rebellious spirit" and the positive attitude towards life advocated in the work gradually recognize the reality in interpersonal interactions, showing an image of a woman who is gentle on the outside but strong on the inside.

My Rating

★★★★☆

Pros: Powerful critique of social problems; the first play to honestly depict modern domestic life and gender dynamics.

Cons: May be biased or not fully reflect the truth in all circumstances, though still broadly applicable.

Final Thoughts

"A Doll's House" stands as a seminal work that challenges the very fabric of societal norms and personal relationships. It encapsulates the essence of individualism and the quest for self-actualization against the backdrop of a patriarchal society. Nora's departure is a resounding declaration of independence, a symbol of the human spirit's yearning for freedom and authenticity. The play remains a poignant reminder of the ongoing struggle for equality and the enduring power of the individual to redefine their own destiny.
