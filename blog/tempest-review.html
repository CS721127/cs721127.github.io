The Tempest:
The Notion of "Self" and "Others" under Caliban

Colonialism, power, and humanity in Shakespeare's final play

Sep 29, 2024· By Cen· Book Reviews

Introduction

As part of my English Literature class, I recently had the pleasure of reading "The Tempest" by Shakespeare. This famous play, written in 1611, continues to captivate readers with its clever wordplay, social satire, and reflection on worldwide problems of its time.

"O brave new world, That has such people in't!"
— Miranda, The Tempest

Plot Overview

The play is centered around Prospero, the exiled Duke of Milan, who uses his magical powers to orchestrate a storm that shipwrecks his enemies on a remote island. With the help of his spirit servant Ariel, Prospero seeks to right the wrongs done to him by his brother Antonio and the King of Naples. As the play progresses, relationships are tested, and characters are forced to confront their past actions, leading to a complex exploration of power, forgiveness, and redemption.

Themes

  • The idea of colonization and dispossession
  • The notion of "Self" and "Other"
  • Speaks eloquently to late twentieth-century readers
  • Compare and contrast between nobles and natives
  • Mirror and parodies of other characters

Who is Caliban?

The "Other." Prospero's reluctant slave, the offspring of the sorceress Sycorax, who once governed the island until her demise before Prospero's arrival. Caliban holds the belief that he is the rightful ruler of the land. Initially, Caliban extended a hand of friendship to Prospero, who in turn provided him with an education. However, Caliban soon realized that Prospero would never regard him as anything more than a civilized barbarian. Despite his capacity for emotional depth and eloquence, Caliban is filled with rage and resentment, and his sole desire is to be free from Prospero's control. The name Caliban is almost an anagram for "cannibal," and in many respects, he represents the indigenous peoples encountered by European colonizers.

Who is Prospero?

The "Self." He is the rightful Duke of Milan, yet was usurped by his brother Antonio and King Alonso of Naples, leading to his exile to a deserted island with his daughter Miranda. Over the course of 12 years, Prospero has transformed into a formidable enchanter, wielding control over the spirit Ariel and the native Caliban. Critics often liken Prospero to a playwright, as he manipulates the lives of those around him much like an author controls characters in a narrative. In a pivotal act of forgiveness, he relinquishes his magic and opts for reconciliation with his adversaries.

Analysis — "Self" and "Other" Under Caliban's Character

The character of Caliban reflects Shakespeare's idea of colonizing and colonized. When Shakespeare wrote his play in 1611, the world was centered on Europe and started expanding from there to other parts of the world. When Prospero "takest [the island] from [Caliban]," he treats Caliban as a "subject" he owns, enslaving him without any freedoms. It is not only his subjugating of Caliban's island but also deeming Caliban as "others" and him as a "self." This difference stemmed from their different power — the magic — just like the colonizers from Europe have the power of guns, epitomizing the long-lasting relationship between "settlers" and "Indians."

The character of Caliban demonstrates the humanity behind his ugly appearance. The common agreement at the time that the "self" always represents civilized while "other" is savage has become obscured with the humanity that Caliban exhibits, challenging this popular certainty about self and others. He learned how to curse from Prospero. While it is true that Caliban is a native and hasn't been educated before Prospero comes, he certainly has a capacity for civilization learned from the colonizer — the complexity of his language.

Caliban parodies other characters, mirroring from "others" to the "self." Caliban's island had been taken by Prospero while his dukedom had been taken by Antonio, indicating the commonality between "self" and "others," that "self" is not a privilege to "others" yet "others" is not inferior to the "self." The sequence of compare and contrast between Caliban and other characters obscures the differences, strengthening mobility and normality between "self" and "others."

Modern Relevance

"The Tempest" remains a powerful commentary on modern issues like mobility and colonialism's legacy. Caliban's plight mirrors indigenous struggles worldwide, highlighting cultural identity complexities. The play also addresses environmental stewardship, a pressing concern in our era of ecological crises. Shakespeare's exploration of these themes ensures "The Tempest" remains relevant, sparking discussions about control, reconciliation, and our responsibility to nature.

My Rating

★★★★★

Pros: Extremely well known; profound meanings for later generations; Shakespeare's last play.

Cons: Old text, may be hard to read throughout; for someone who does not like reading plays might be a challenge.

Final Thoughts

"The Tempest" reflects on multiple sides of the issues that Shakespeare was trying to address, including colonization, rebellion, and mobility, which are all great topics to notice while reading it. However, due to the space limit, this blog only covers Caliban as main focus. Really recommend interpreting this play from other perspectives!
