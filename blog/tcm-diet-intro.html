Traditional Chinese Medicine:
What it Suggests about Food and Diet

An introduction to TCM dietary philosophy

Posted Jan 7, 2024· By Cen· Culture Share

Yao Shi Tong Yuan — Food and Medicine Are One

Traditional Chinese Medicine (TCM) is a holistic system of health and wellness that has been practiced in China for thousands of years. TCM has a significant emphasis on the role of food and diet. It is based on the concept of Qi (气, pronounced "chee"), which is the vital energy that flows through the body, and the balance of Yin and Yang inside the human body, aiming to maintain or restore harmony by ensuring the smooth flow of Qi.

The Cold 寒 and Warm 热

In TCM, food is considered a natural form of medicine, called 药食同源 (Yao Shi Tong Yuan). Each food item has its own unique properties that can influence the body in different ways. For example, ginger is known for its warming effects, which can help dispel cold and improve blood circulation, making it suitable for those with a cold constitution. On the other hand, mung beans have cooling and detoxifying properties, making them ideal for summer consumption to alleviate internal heat and toxins.

Yin and Yang Foods

Yin and Yang and the Five Elements

Yin and Yang are central concepts in TCM, and foods are categorized based on their Yin or Yang attributes. Yin foods typically have cooling, moistening, and astringent characteristics — usually associated with cold 寒 — such as fruits like pears, apples, and bananas, as well as vegetables, tofu, and kelp. These foods are suitable for hot seasons or individuals with internal heat, helping to lower body temperature and alleviate symptoms of dryness.

Yang foods, with their warming, invigorating, and dispersing properties, include meats like beef, chicken, and lamb, as well as spices like ginger, garlic, and chili. These foods are ideal for cold seasons or individuals with a Yang deficiency, as they can warm the body and enhance Yang energy. By properly combining Yin and Yang foods, one can achieve a balanced state of health — that is the goal of healthy eating by TCM's definition.

The Five Elements and Five Flavors

The Five Fundamental Elements in TCM

TCM correlates the Five Elements (wood, fire, earth, metal, and water) with the Five Flavors (sour, bitter, sweet, pungent, and salty) and associates them with the body's Five Organs (liver, heart, spleen, lungs, and kidneys):

  • Sour → Liver: Foods like hawthorn and dried plums regulate emotions and alleviate fatigue.
  • Bitter → Heart: Bitter melon and buckwheat address excessive heart fire and insomnia.
  • Sweet → Spleen: Dates, honey, and pumpkin strengthen the spleen and boost immunity.
  • Pungent → Lungs: Onions, ginger, and garlic relieve coughs and prevent colds.
  • Salty → Kidneys: Kelp and nori address kidney deficiency and back pain.

Eating in Harmony with the Seasons

The Unity of Heaven and Man (天人合一)

TCM emphasizes "unity of heaven and man (天人合一)," suggesting that the human body is closely connected to nature, and diet should align with the changing seasons:

  • Spring: Green leafy vegetables, bean sprouts, and strawberries promote liver function and metabolism.
  • Summer: Cooling foods like watermelon, cucumber, and mung bean soup replenish fluids.
  • Autumn: Pears, apples, sesame seeds, and honey moisten the lungs against dryness.
  • Winter: Beef, chicken, black beans, and walnuts enhance Yang energy and warmth.

Moderation in Diet

Moderate Diet (饮食节制)

TCM advocates for "moderation in diet (饮食节制)," emphasizing that excessive or insufficient eating can have adverse effects on health. Overeating can burden the spleen and stomach, leading to indigestion and obesity. It is recommended to eat until about 70% full, which satisfies the body's nutritional needs without overburdening the digestive system. Maintaining regular meal times and avoiding binge eating supports the body's normal functions and health.

TCM also focuses on individualized treatment, recognizing that each person's constitution, age, gender, and living environment are different. For example, individuals with a qi deficiency should consume foods that have invigorating effects, such as astragalus, ginseng, and yam. Those with a damp-heat constitution should eat foods with heat-clearing effects, like mung beans and bitter melon.

These are my brief introduction to what Traditional Chinese Medicine suggests about our daily diet and food selection. I hope this can help you to have a healthy life!
